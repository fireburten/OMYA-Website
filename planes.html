Those Planes That Fly
Too Damn Low

An evidence-based platform for proving, conclusively, that yes — those planes are flying too low.

The problem was simple: planes are flying too damn low over residential areas, too frequently, and at antisocial hours. The question was how to prove it.

This project ingests ADS-B flight data from a local receiver, cleans and enriches it with route and aircraft metadata, then surfaces it through a live Superset dashboard — with altitude distributions, frequency heatmaps, and worst-offender rankings.

The Problem

Most flight-tracking tools are built for enthusiasts, not complainers. None of them let you ask "how often does something fly under 2,000ft over my exact location between 6am and 8am on weekdays?" This does.

The Approach

  • Deployed a Raspberry Pi with an SDR receiver to capture ADS-B transponder signals
  • Ingested raw data into PostgreSQL via a Python ETL pipeline running on a schedule
  • Enriched with aircraft registration data and route lookups
  • Built a Superset dashboard with altitude filters, time-of-day slices, and a heat map

What I Found

The data confirmed what the ears already knew. Peak low-altitude activity was between 7:15am and 8:30am, with a secondary spike around 5pm. Three operators accounted for 60% of all sub-2,000ft passes.

What I Learned

  • ADS-B data is messier than it looks — deduplication and trajectory reconstruction took most of the pipeline time
  • Superset's SQL Lab is genuinely excellent for rapid iteration on dashboard queries
  • Having the data doesn't automatically win the argument — but it helps
